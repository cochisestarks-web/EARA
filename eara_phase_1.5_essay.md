Section 1: Personal Motivation
Nobody told me the schedule was the weapon. That lesson came through the “clopen” — a closing shift followed by an opening shift with a very short turnaround. When I first worked retail, my shift would start at 2–3 p.m., was supposed to end at 10 p.m., but often ran to 11 p.m. or 1 a.m. Then I’d be expected back by 4–5 a.m. the next morning, commuting on minimal sleep and no real recovery. The system treated this as normal, but it felt abusive and dangerous.

These clopen schedules became abusive also because the store manager was “friends” with my manager (my high school buddy). That friendship turned into a privilege he felt comfortable abusing. When my friend called him out on the scheduling, the response was classic patriarchal shaming — insults about his resolve and masculinity. Now looking back on it, it reads like a TV‑show drama, but at the time it was just another day at work. After days, maybe weeks, of similar shifts, the toll took over, and my friend eventually quit for a better opportunity and more money.

Fast forward 3–4 years, and I found myself in the same boat. By then I was working at Sprouts, a public company with more formal policies. Clopens still happened to me, but less frequently. When they did, they were devastating — the body would be in this liminal zone between waking and sleep, a physiological whiplash.

For me, it was never the clopens that affected me most — it was the inconsistent, chaotic scheduling. The “you signed up for this” or “this is just the way the industry works” lines were everywhere. I know logically why it happens — staffing, understaffing, the fact that everyone in every industry is understaffed — but that doesn’t make it less draining.

The schedules were hard to plan around. During my time at Sprouts, I rarely had the flexibility to see a doctor without cutting into what little time I had to myself. I could schedule appointments in the morning before work, but that ate up my decompression time — the few minutes I had to breathe, rest, or recover. Why would I want to spend my off‑hours in doctor’s offices?

At the same time, I was often scheduled for closing shifts because it was more convenient for the other managers. Sprouts did try to address some of this — they noticed that “4th managers” (customer service managers) were quitting at a higher rate and had a big operational gap compared to other store roles. That’s when I started paying attention: the schedule wasn’t just a roster; it was a management tool, and for a lot of people, it was a weapon.

Earlier, as a stock clerk at another retailer, several times during COVID, I’d experienced split shifts — coming in at 8 a.m., then leaving at 12 p.m. because a truck hadn’t shown — and being told, “Just stay ready when we call or text you.” You’re on‑call, but unpaid, and the schedule is invisible. In right‑to‑work states, this was common.

In retail, schedules were often posted with little or no notice, leaving the next week up in the air despite requested time off. Back 20 years ago there was more predictability. Now we’re on PTO systems, which have their pros and cons, but the lack of advance notice made it hard to plan family events or even doctor’s appointments.

And then there were “resets” — overnight store‑rearrangements starting at 10 p.m. and sometimes running until 6 or 7 a.m. Sometimes you’d get a heads‑up weeks in advance, but more often it was a last‑minute call‑in. You’d show up, only to learn, “You’re doing a reset,” and your entire schedule was thrown out. The only time you really got a heads-up was when the reset was at your own store.

Two call‑ins sealed it for me at Sprouts. The first time, I had planned a bowling outing with my son and wife. We had to cut it short because I was called in. She understood, but it made me sad — sad for my son, whose time with his dad wasn’t being valued.

The next week, it happened again. Same day, same event, same result. That’s when I realized something was wrong with the system, not just with me. Why wasn’t my time valuable? Why was my company’s time worth more than mine? Didn’t I generate their value?

That’s when I started paying attention. The schedule wasn’t just making me tired. It was stealing capacity — the ability to recover, think clearly, show up as a whole person instead of a depleted shell.

The question wasn’t new. I’d been carrying it since those bowling days with my son — seven years earlier. I just didn’t have the tools to answer it yet.

For years, I didn’t even know I could Google “wage labor” or search for economics terms. I knew the stock market — I held stocks, I understood financial instruments — but labor economics felt like a foreign language I wasn’t allowed to speak. I heard about UBI through Andrew Yang’s campaign, heard whispers about AI, but it all felt distant. I was just trying to survive.

Then 2024 arrived, and I started playing with AI daily. My knowledge became more well-rounded. NotebookLM searches started reflecting economic interests I didn’t even know I had. I finally had the tools to investigate.

And then 2025 hit, and everything kind of hit at once.

My mom died. My team leader was getting married. One of our workers got injured and never came back. My dairy clerk — overworked, frustrated — told me he just wanted more time with his wife and son. And then a guy I’d never really gotten along with, someone I’d had drama with for years, said something I’ll never forget:

“I think six hours is my limit. That sounds like a good number for work.”

I told him: “Hell yeah.”

That conversation sparked me to Google about the Scandinavian experiments we’d been hearing about. And after so many conversations like that — people voicing what I’d been feeling for years — I just wanted to show them they were right about their instincts. I wanted to prove it. Not just morally, but financially. Because I know how businesses work. I wanted to prove that keeping the old model, holding the status quo, was costing them money.

I researched whether there was a biological sweet spot — a Goldilocks zone for work hours. I was right. There is.

And — I’ll keep it honest — out of pure spite, I added the financial proof. I wanted to show them the cost in dollars, not just in human suffering.

That’s when I started building the tool.

Section 2: Ghost Hours
I named this threshold the “Point of No Return,” or less dramatically, the moment a worker hits their biological “wall.” In the simulation, the system flags this as “Ghost Hours” — the time when the body has hit its limits and is running on fumes, not full capacity.

The first time I experienced Ghost Hours was during an overnight inventory shift at 11 p.m. to 8 a.m. By 4 a.m., I was driving myself home. The road curved, the parking lot curved, and I misjudged it just enough that my car struck a curb at speed. The impact popped a tire. I’m not sure whether I nodded off or just miscalculated the turn, but I know I was past the edge of safe cognitive function.

That’s when I understood: Ghost Hours weren’t abstract. They were a safety risk, a physiological line I’d crossed without even realizing it.

Later, as a closing‑shift manager, I’d drive home after long nights and find myself drifting in and out of sleep on the highway, corrected by the sheer adrenaline of almost swerving. The Ghost Hours weren’t just about long shifts. They were about the accumulated toll — the compounding effect of disrupted circadian rhythms, too‑little sleep, and constant recovery‑debt.

The short turnarounds — the clopens, the late‑closing, the early‑opening — didn’t just steal time. They stole the few hours of awake time I had with my family. I often had less than five hours to be present, and they were the only time my body felt safe enough to rest. When I came home around 1–2 p.m., my son would want to play video games, and I’d fall asleep during the game. He’d nudge or kick my foot, and I’d wake up, ashamed that I was, in effect, using him as a wake‑alarm.

That’s when I realized my body wasn’t just tired. It was trying to recover on the fly, in the middle of what should have been quality time with my son.

Eventually, I asked my manager, “With all the sleep we miss, do you think we’ll ever make it back?” He just looked at me, confused, as if the idea that sleep debt could be permanent hadn’t occurred to him. That’s when I knew the system didn’t just ignore the cost. It designed it out of the calculations.

That’s when I realized the schedule wasn’t just a work plan. It was a system that treated my body, my time, and my capacity to recover as endless raw material.

Section 3: Building EARA and the Oasis Blind Spot
I needed quantifiable measurements — something that could track degradation over time, not just point-in-time exhaustion. Not just “I’m tired after this shift” — but accumulation. Your body doesn’t reset overnight. A clopen doesn’t just wreck one day — it compounds. The debt builds.

The research led me to Darwish’s work on optimal workday length — but that wasn’t enough. I needed to understand why a workday has limits. That led me to Borbély’s two-process model: sleep debt accumulates based on how long you’re awake (Process S) and where you are in your circadian rhythm (Process C).

That’s when High Octane officially became something else. From Borbély on, I had moved from a scheduling optimizer to full biological accountability.

I kept digging. I found research on HRV suppression — heart rate variability dropping under sustained stress. That gave me the physiological marker. I found Van Dongen’s work on sleep restriction and cognitive performance degradation. Together, these gave me a foundation: I could track biological stress and fatigue accumulation over time.

That’s when I built gamma (γ) — a metric that tracks biological debt accumulation. It’s dimensionless, scaled 0 to 1.0. Zero means no debt. 0.3 is optimal. 0.7 is the biological ceiling — the point where your body can’t sustain output anymore. 1.0 is shutdown. I developed it from Borbély’s two-process model and Enoka’s work on neuromechanical fatigue accumulation.

I didn’t know it yet, but I was operationalizing chronoeconomics — the study of how time-based extraction operates in economic systems. I just knew I needed to measure what the schedule was stealing, and I needed concrete evidence backed by biological data. I called it EARA — Employee Adaptive Resource Allocation. What started as High Octane, a scheduling optimizer, had become a biological accountability system.

Then I found the Oasis blind spot.

From my time at Sprouts, I knew our scheduling system used a formula similar to Oasis, the workforce‑management tool used by Publix and other retailers. It computes productivity as a ratio of hours given to hours worked, then maps that to sales output. Management sees these numbers and treats them as a measure of efficiency: if a department is “underperforming,” it’s presumed to be inefficient.

The problem? Oasis assumes 100% biological capacity for every scheduled hour.

It cannot see Ghost Hours — the time when a worker is clocked in but functioning at a fraction of capacity. It also cannot see managerial overhead: hours spent on paperwork, scheduling, forecasting, and administrative work still count, in the formula, as “productive floor labor.”

That creates a structural blind spot — and likely not only in Oasis. Based on my experience with multiple workforce management systems across retailers, this mechanism appears to be a common design assumption.

Workers can be working themselves past exhaustion, and the system will still label them “inefficient.” The more depleted they get, the more the formula blames them — and the more management justifies further cuts, understaffing, or schedule‑intensification.

That’s where the Biological Capacity Index (BCI) comes in.

BCI = 1 − γ, where γ is a worker’s accumulated biological debt (0–1.0, scaled so 0.3 is optimal, 0.7 is near‑ceiling, and 1.0 is shutdown).

If a worker produces 80% output at BCI 0.40, their adjusted productivity is 200% of their available biological capacity. They’re not underperforming. They’re overperforming — under conditions that are quietly eroding their health.

That’s the mechanism of illegibility. The system misreads biological extraction as inefficiency, then uses that misreading to justify more extraction.

My goal wasn’t to critique the system from the outside. It was to expose the blind spot that already existed in the system’s own measurement logic.

Section 4: Validation, Limitations, and Phase 1.5
I accounted for the gaps in EARA responsibly, given that I’m an independent researcher with no lab, no institution, no formal funding.

During early development, I used AI-assisted research tools to identify potential overlaps with existing literature and pressure-test internal consistency — not as scientific validation, but as conceptual critique when I lacked academic colleagues.

I validated γ against real data. I tested it against WESAD — a controlled lab study of 15 subjects under episodic stress — and found correlations with HRV suppression and fatigue scores around r ~0.5. The accumulation rates align closely with Van Dongen’s sleep‑restriction data (r ~0.999). That doesn’t prove EARA works in the real world, but it proves the underlying logic is sound.

What EARA currently lacks:

Chronotype stratification: it assumes a standard circadian rhythm, not accounting for night‑owls, shift‑workers, or people whose natural clocks clash with their schedules.
Hysteresis: it assumes linear recovery; it doesn’t model long‑term damage from chronic depletion.
Mental‑health covariates: depression, anxiety, PTSD, or other clinical states that affect biological capacity.
Edge cases: recovery naps, high‑adrenaline roles, or gig‑economy‑style stacking of part‑time schedules.
Phase 2 will measure whether chronic scheduling creates permanent baseline shifts in γ — biological costs that short-term recovery cannot reverse.

I know this isn’t the first framework to study temporal precarity, schedule‑unpredictability, or worker‑debt. It’s a novel synthesis — bringing together circadian‑biology, biological‑depletion metrics, and workforce‑management‑blind‑spots into an operational tool.

I’m publishing now for three reasons:

To establish priority and timestamp the framework.
To invite peer review from people who can pressure‑test it on better data.
To open collaboration with researchers who can help me move EARA into Phase 2 — a 30‑worker, 28‑day cohort study in a live retail environment, with chronotype stratification, mental‑health covariates, and HRV monitoring.
This is Phase 1.5: a solid, documented framework with transparent limitations. The invitation is open.

Section 5: Why This Matters — Austerity, Illegibility, and the Chrono-Tax
I built EARA independently — I wanted to preserve my own synthesis before reading what others had written. Once the framework was solid, I looked further. I wondered: Had anyone else tried measuring time-based extraction?

That’s when I found chronoeconomics — the study of how time operates as a site of economic extraction. Scholars like Mark Rifkin (temporal sovereignty) and Clara Mattei (austerity as labor discipline) had been mapping this terrain for years. I hadn’t invented a field — I’d converged with one.

Clara Mattei’s 10-year study on austerity maps the macro framework I’d been building toward independently: austerity operates through illegibility — making extraction invisible so it can continue without accountability. Labor discipline isn’t imposed through force. It’s imposed through policy mechanisms that make non-compliance more costly than submission. The worker absorbs the endpoint of that cascade biologically.

The Oasis discovery showed me what I was looking at: operational illegibility — austerity imposed through scheduling policy. Management’s productivity formulas discipline workers the same way austerity disciplines labor — by systematically misreading biological depletion as inefficiency, then justifying further cuts based on that misreading. EARA exposes the mechanism. It shows how austerity’s logic — illegibility, labor discipline through policy — operates at the scheduling and biological level.

Corporations — especially in retail — extract biological capacity through scheduling policies. But they don’t declare rates, they don’t track costs, and they evade accountability. I call this the chrono-tax — an undeclared levy on workers’ biological reserves. EARA makes it visible. It turns temporal extraction into a measurable, auditable variable.

Where Mattei’s work maps macro-level austerity policy, EARA maps the same illegibility mechanism at the scheduling and biological level — inside the store, inside the shift, inside the body.

Section 6: Call to Collaboration
I’m Derek. I’ve spent 20 years on the retail floor, and this is the first time I’ve tried to write all of this down.

I’m reaching out to Clara Mattei, Grace Blakeley, Gary Stevenson, and Dr. Diane Boivin because EARA operationalizes mechanisms you’ve been mapping — austerity as illegibility (Mattei), temporal extraction as political discipline (Blakeley), wealth inequality as a systemic tool to self perpetuate its own means (Stevenson) and circadian disruption as biological cost (Boivin). Where your work focuses on macro-level policy and systemic extraction, EARA traces the same mechanisms at the scheduling level — inside workforce management infrastructure, inside the shift, inside the worker’s body.

This is Phase 1.5 — a documented framework with preliminary validation and transparent limitations. I can’t run a prospective cohort study on my own. I can’t control for chronotype without a research team. I can’t build hysteresis models without access to longitudinal data. That’s why I’m reaching out.

What Phase 2 needs: A prospective 30-worker retail cohort study over 28 days. Chronotype stratification. Mental health covariates. HRV monitoring. Schedule-variance controls. Real-world validation in a live retail environment.

The broader vision: EARA isn’t just about retail scheduling. It’s a rinse-and-repeat methodology for exposing extraction mechanisms wherever temporal control operates — insurance denial cycles, benefits bureaucracy delays, gig economy algorithmic fragmentation. One case study at a time. Retail first — the one that affected me personally. Then the rest.

If this aligns with your research priorities, I’d welcome the opportunity to discuss how we might validate and refine it together.

I’m a first-time independent researcher with 20 years on the retail floor. I’ve watched these schedules wear people down in ways that never showed up in the data. This is my attempt to turn that experience into a framework that can sit alongside academic work — not to replace it, but to add the floor-level perspective that’s been missing.
