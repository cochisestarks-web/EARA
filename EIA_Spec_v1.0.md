Extraction Index Architecture (γ) — Specification v1.0
A cross-domain measurement architecture for institutional extraction

Purpose
The Extraction Index Architecture defines a reusable, dimensionless index γ ∈ [0,1] for quantifying hidden depletion caused by institutional processes across biological, financial, and social systems.
γ is not a theory of extraction but a measurement architecture: it operationalizes depletion as measurable, threshold-defined states ranging from recoverable debt (γ < 0.7) to irreversible degradation (γ ≥ 0.7), enabling cross-domain comparison and policy intervention.

Core Components
γ-form (accumulation function)
A modular function that maps domain-specific inputs (e.g., shift sequences, payment delays, denial cycles, algorithmic nudges) onto a 0–1 depletion state. The form remains constant (weighted composite of stressor exposure + recovery deficit), but the inputs and weights are domain-calibrated.
Threshold logic
Four interpretable bands with distinct policy implications:

0–0.3: Safe / optimal (sustainable operations)
0.3–0.7: Stress / warning (intervention recommended)
0.7–1.0: Critical / extraction (irreversible harm zone)
1.0: Effective shutdown (complete reserve depletion)

Domain-specific inputs
Each instantiation defines what depletes reserves in that system:

Circadian misalignment and temporal fragmentation (retail scheduling)
Bureaucratic denial-delay cycles (insurance, welfare systems)
Algorithmic speed-ups and task fragmentation (platform work)
Resource drawdown faster than regeneration (ecological, financial)

Validation pipeline
A protocol that links γ outputs to observable outcomes (e.g., HRV suppression, claim abandonment rates, burnout markers, turnover) to anchor the index in empirical behavior. Each instantiation must demonstrate statistically robust γ-threshold correspondence with domain-specific harm markers before deployment.

Key Properties
Dimensionless (0–1)
Enables cross-domain comparison: a γ = 0.6 in scheduling, insurance, and gig work can be interpreted as comparably severe depletion states.
Composite
Integrates multiple stressors into a single index without requiring a single "unit" of harm (e.g., time, money, or biological load).
Threshold-defined
Produces discrete policy bands, not just a smooth scale, so institutions can attach concrete rules and safeguards.
Asymmetric recovery
Depletion accumulates faster than reserves regenerate. γ is explicitly designed to model the rigidity of institutional harm: crossing critical thresholds (γ ≥ 0.7) triggers degradation that persists even after stressors are removed.
Debt-to-degradation transition
At low thresholds (γ < 0.3), depletion manifests as recoverable debt. At critical thresholds (γ ≥ 0.7), depletion becomes structural degradation—irreversible harm to regulatory systems, health, or social infrastructure.

Instantiations (current and planned)
EARA v1.0 (Employee Adaptive Resource Allocation)
Biological depletion via irregular scheduling. Operationalizes γ through sleep debt (Borbély Process S), circadian misalignment penalties, and shift sequence analysis. Validated against HRV suppression, fatigue scores, and recovery deficits (WESAD dataset, n=15, r > 0.5***). Applied to retail workforce management systems (Oasis) to expose "Ghost Hours"—scheduled labor hours at depleted biological capacity.
[Planned] Insurance/welfare extraction
γ encoded into denial-delay cycles, eligibility churn, and bureaucratic friction. Measures financial and cognitive depletion via claim abandonment rates and administrative burden.
[Planned] Gig-platform extraction
γ mapped onto algorithmically driven task fragmentation, speed-ups, and on-demand volatility. Measures temporal and attentional depletion via response-time degradation and exit rates.

When citing this specification, you reference not merely a metric but a generalizable architecture for institutional extraction. This enables outreach across disciplines:

"I've built a generalized measurement architecture for quantifying institutional extraction. EARA is its first instantiation in retail scheduling, validated against physiological stress markers. The framework is designed to travel—the same γ-logic can measure extraction in insurance denials, gig platforms, welfare bureaucracies, or any system that depletes reserves faster than they regenerate."

This positions γ as methodological infrastructure, not domain-specific critique, and opens pathways for collaboration, adaptation, and cross-domain validation.
