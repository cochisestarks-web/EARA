Asset-stripping isn't only financial. Every schedule that forces a worker through a circadian trough, cuts recovery time, or sequences shifts to maximize hours also extracts something that doesn't appear in any productivity dashboard: biological capacity. EARA makes that extraction measurable.

The Problem

Standard workforce analytics assume 100% biological capacity per hour worked. They cannot see fatigue accumulation, circadian misalignment, or the compounding cost of shift sequencing. The result: productivity ceilings that look like worker underperformance are actually biological debt made invisible by the model doing the measuring.

The Framework

EARA (Employee Adaptive Resource Allocation) introduces γ (gamma) — a dimensionless biological debt index grounded in Borbély's two-process sleep model and Enoka's neuromechanical fatigue accumulation. γ runs 0 to 1.0 and converts directly into BCI: Biological Capacity Index = (1 − γ).

Core Formula — γ-debt
γ_debt(t) = Σ [ α · S(t) + β · C_penalty(t) ]
α = 0.6 — homeostatic sleep pressure weight (Process S)
β = 0.4 — circadian misalignment penalty weight (Process C)
S(t) — Borbély Process S value at shift end
C_penalty — circadian trough exposure, capped at 6-hr ceiling

γ Thresholds

0.0 — No debt 0.3 — Optimal ceiling 0.7 — Biological ceiling 1.0 — Shutdown

The Oasis Discovery. Retail workforce management system Oasis has two structural blind spots: it assumes 100% biological capacity per scheduled hour (cannot see Ghost Hours), and it counts managerial overhead as floor-productive labor — "headcount laundering." Field observation: 85–90% productivity ceiling despite under-scheduling. EARA's BCI formula — Adjusted Productivity = Output × (1 − γ) — converts this from theoretical critique into a live diagnostic tool.

Beyond Retail — The Chrono-Tax Audit

EARA is a rinse-and-repeat methodology. Retail scheduling is Case Study #1. The same extraction logic applies to insurance-denial cycles, benefits-bureaucracy delays, gig-economy schedule fragmentation, and criminal-justice scheduling — any system that strips biological recovery time as a structural feature.
